A surprising link between electrophysiological data in monkeys and a behavioral model in humans¶

Preamble¶

With Thibault Gajdos, Boris Burle and F.-Xavier Alario we recently published a preprint titled "The Decisive Role of Non-Decision Time for Interpreting the Parameters of Decision Making Models". As in any article some choices had to be made and what I am presenting in this post was not the focus of the article plus the analysis was unplanned and therefore should be taken with a grain of salt. But I still wanted to share this result as I find it extremely interesting.

In the study reported in the preprint, using experimental manipulations and an electrophysiological measure of response execution, we assessed the ability of a decision-making model to account for latent cognitive processes in a perceptual decision task.

In this task we manipulated the amount of force to produce a response, speed accuracy trade-off instructions and finally stimulus contrast. This last manipulation had an interesting property that revealed a surprising pattern but let's first review the task of the participants and some of the results from the study.

The task¶

Participants had to indicate which of two sinusoidal gratings, left and right of a fixation cross, had the most contrast in it with the corresponding button press :

Now the contrast manipulation consisted in decreasing the overall contrast of both stimuli while maintaining their difference constant :

Given what we know about properties of the visual system, higher contrast as on the left of the image yields faster brain response even in the earliest visual sensory systems compared to dimmer stimuli as on the extrem right part of the image. The nice thing about that manipulation is that, according to Weber-Fechner law, the difficulty in comparing the gratings decreases with the decrease in brightness wich can be seen on the behavioral performance, participants make more errors when contrast increases and their reaction times ($RT$) slows down (see the preprint for details of the figure) :

Hence we have here a manipulation where you expect that the time for visual encoding of the stimulus evolves inversly to the time you need to decide. The overall measure of RT cannot show you both effects as they are cancelling each other, ideally we would have a cognitive model of behavior accounting for both decision and encoding times. In our paper we tested amongst others, whether the well-known Drift Diffusion Model (as implemented by Ratcliff, R., \& Tuerlinckx, F., 2002) captures simultaneously the decision and visual properties of this manipulation.

Initial results¶

The increase in decision time is obviously captured by the model given the high impact of the increase in contrast on both participants accuracy and $RT$s. More interesting is that the model is also able to capture the fact that the parameter assumed to relate to encoding of the stimulus is decreasing with the increase in contrast. The following figure illustrates that the centered ($x_i - \overline{x}$) parameter for encoding time (y-axis) is indeed decreasing with contrast increase (x-axis).

A surprising link¶

Now one thing is particularly interesting. We do see that there is some kind of non-linearity here. I started to show this to scientists with more expertise in visual processing. At that occasion I was redirected to an article written in 2012 by Reynaud, Masson and Chavane (https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.1618-12.2012). In their article they analyze, using voltage-sensitive dye, the temporal dynamics of visual neuron in the primary visual cortex of monkeys (Macaca mulata) in response to a manipulation of the contrast of the gratings. In one of their analysis (see Figure 5B. of their article) they report measurment of onset of activity in V1 neurons in response to different contrasts. Using the Naka-Rushton equation, a well known equation luminance-response function, in its inverted version (Barthelemy, Fleuriet \& Masson, 2010) they provide a description of the onset of activity in V1 in response to different contrast levels.

Therefore, the next step is easy, we just had to recover the values predicted for the contrast levels we used in our study from the fit of the inverted Naka-Rushton equation and compare that to what the model we used estimated based on behavior alone. At that time it was really just out of curiosity but...

def Naka_Rushton(par,contrast,maxAmp, maxShift):#Inverted Naka-rushton equation from Barthélemy, Fleuriet and Masson 2010
    c50, slope = par[0],par[1]
    return (maxAmp + maxShift * ((contrast**slope)/((c50**slope)+(contrast**slope))))

# Hereby we obtain the prediction of onset of neurons in V1 for a given set of contrast from 23 to 93% as we used in our study
# To see how we recovered the parameters see https://github.com/GWeindel/The-Decisive-Role-of-Non-Decision-Time/blob/main/3-DDM_analysis.ipynb
predicted_V1 = Naka_Rushton(par=[11.42,-1.54] , maxAmp = 36.99, maxShift=51.66,
                                                contrast=np.linspace(23,93,100))

plt.figure(figsize=(5,4), dpi=150)
plt.plot(np.linspace(23,93.1,100), predicted_V1, color="green")
plt.xlabel('Contrast levels (%)')
plt.ylabel('V1 predicted neuron response time (ms)');

Damned that looks close !! What if we superpose both ? Well we can do that, but obviously V1 neurons respond really fast and visual processing goes on beyond the first response latency. Therefore we need to be on the same scale, we can do this be simply subtracting the mean of each estimate (human behavioral model vs V1 measurment) to each data point ($x_i - \overline{x}$) so that we only keep the relative difference of each point to the mean of each variable

Wow... That is impressive. A behavioral model fitted on data from a decision making task performed by humans is capturing visual encoding latencies that very closely match actual measurment of visual neurons in monkeys !

Now the story doesn't end here This congruence for example onlyu happens when we particularly stree participants to respond as fast as possible. When we swith to an accuracy-focused condition, things do really not look the same :
